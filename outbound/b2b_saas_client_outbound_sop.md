## 1. The Philosophy

Before writing a single word of copy, you must understand what cold outreach actually is. Most people get this wrong. Getting it wrong means writing sequences that nobody replies to.

### 1.1 What Cold Outreach Actually Is

Cold outreach is not marketing. It is not advertising. It is not a broadcast.

Cold outreach is a one-to-one human conversation that happens to start without permission.

The moment it stops feeling like a conversation and starts feeling like a pitch, you have lost. The recipient's brain pattern-matches it to the 50 other pitches they got this week and ignores it.

> **The only goal of outreach is to get a reply. Not a demo. Not a sale. A reply.**

Everything else — demos, pilots, closed deals — comes after the reply. If you optimize for demos before optimizing for replies, you will get neither.

### 1.2 Why Most Outreach Fails

Most outreach fails for one of five reasons:

- It leads with the product, not the person. Nobody cares about your tool. They care about their problem.
- It sounds like it was written by a marketing team. Decision-makers get 20 of these a day. They smell the template instantly.
- It tries to do too much in one message. One message, one job. Always.
- It begs instead of pulls. Desperation is repellent. Confidence is magnetic.
- It pitches too early. You have not earned the right to pitch yet. Earn it first.

### 1.3 Teja's Core Belief About Outreach

> *"Break the pattern. Make them laugh, make them curious, make them emotional. The goal is a reply. Everything else is noise."*

What this means in practice:

- **Generic = death.** Every line must feel like it was written for this specific person.
- **Formal + fun is the target tone.** Not corporate. Not casual. Somewhere in between.
- **Give value before asking for anything.** The stat, the insight, the observation — give it first.
- **Self-awareness cuts through noise faster than cleverness.** Acknowledge the awkwardness. It disarms people.
- **Short beats long. Always.** Every extra word is a reason to close the tab.
- **Pull, don't push.** Make them feel like they're missing out, not being sold to.

## 2. Teja's Taste

This section is critical for AI agents. It documents exactly what Teja approves and what he rejects — and the pattern behind both. Use this to calibrate every piece of copy before presenting it.

### 2.1 What Teja Approves

#### ✅ Self-aware openers
> *"I'll keep this short because your inbox probably looks like mine."*

Why it works: It acknowledges reality. It makes the sender sound human. It earns the reader's attention before asking for it.

Pattern: Acknowledge the awkwardness of reaching out → deliver value → ask.

---

#### ✅ Specific data before the ask
> *"78% of devs write code faster with agents. Only 21% of teams ship faster."*

Why it works: Gives the recipient something real before asking anything of them. Earns the question that follows.

Pattern: Counterintuitive stat + why it matters to them + easy question.

---

#### ✅ One idea per line
> *"Agents write code fast but lose context faster. Every session resets. Coordination breaks."*

Why it works: Each line lands separately. Nobody gets a wall of text. Mobile-friendly. Scannable.

Pattern: Short declarative sentences. Never more than one idea per line. White space is your friend.

---

#### ✅ Confident CTAs that don't beg
> *"Worth it?" — "Is {{company}} in?" — "I'm one message away."*

Why it works: Binary. Low pressure. Puts the decision on them, not on us.

Pattern: Never ask for a meeting directly in early messages. Ask for a yes or no.

---

#### ✅ Scarcity that's true
> *"We're only working with a handful of teams right now." / "Pilots are closing."*

Why it works: Real scarcity is credible. Fake scarcity gets spotted instantly.

Pattern: Only use scarcity if it's true. Never fabricate urgency.

---

### 2.2 What Teja Rejects — Hard Rules

> **AI AGENTS: These are non-negotiable. Never include any of the following in copy.**

#### ❌ Niche-specific hooks that feel researched
Example of what NOT to write: *"Running Codex and Cursor in parallel, wondering why the sprint still slips"*

Why rejected: It sounds like you Googled them for 5 minutes and dropped in keywords. Decision-makers smell this instantly.

---

#### ❌ Product pitches in DM 1
Example of what NOT to write: *"I build tools for engineering teams, would love to connect."*

Why rejected: The moment you mention the product in DM 1, you become a salesperson. Rapport is dead before it starts.

**Rule: DM 1 contains zero product information. Zero.**

---

#### ❌ Corporate phrases
Never use:
- "I wanted to reach out"
- "I hope this finds you well"
- "Quick question"
- "Revolutionary"
- "Game-changing"
- "Seamless"
- "AI-powered"

---

#### ❌ Introducing the sender/product in DM 1
Example of what NOT to write: *"Hey, I'm [Name], founder of [Product]..."*

Why rejected: DM 1 is about them, not you.

---

#### ❌ Fake social proof
Never fabricate client results. Never present hypothetical outcomes as real ones.

Rule: Use real clients with honest framing. "Working with [Client] right now" is honest. "[Client] saw 3x velocity improvement" without real data is not.

---

#### ❌ Desperate follow-ups
Never write:
- "Just checking in"
- "Did you get a chance to see my last message?"
- "Following up on my previous email"

Why rejected: This is begging. It signals that you need them more than they need you.

---

#### ❌ Paragraphs in DMs
LinkedIn DMs are read on mobile, in notifications, in 10-second windows.

Rule: Every DM must be readable in under 10 seconds. One idea per line. Maximum 4-5 lines total.

## 3. Research Foundation

You cannot write good outreach without deep research. Do not skip any of these steps.

### 3.1 The Five Research Documents

Every outbound sequence requires these five inputs. All five must exist before writing begins.

#### Document 1: Product Deep Dive
- One-line positioning statement
- Core value proposition in buyer language
- Specific outcomes the product produces (not features — outcomes)
- What the product does NOT do (exclusions matter as much as inclusions)
- How onboarding works (this becomes the scarcity/pilot angle)

#### Document 2: ICP Definition
- Primary title(s) — exact LinkedIn title variants
- Company type, stage, size
- Hard filter: what qualifies them
- Hard exclusions: who to explicitly NOT target
- One-line ICP for use in targeting config

#### Document 3: Pain Point Mining
- Top 3 pains in priority order
- Verbatim phrases buyers use to describe their pain
- Trigger events — what makes them start looking for a solution
- Stats and data points that quantify the pain

#### Document 4: Competitive Research
- Primary competitor and their headline
- Messaging gaps — what nobody in the market is saying
- Table stakes — what every competitor claims (do not lead with these)
- Client's specific differentiator vs each major competitor

#### Document 5: Positioning Angles
- Primary angle (the hero — the one line everything else supports)
- Supporting angles for different sequence steps
- Copy principles: what to always say, what to never say
- The single strongest stat to lead with

---

### 3.2 Sender Voice Card

Build this before writing a single word of copy. This card governs every touchpoint.

```
Sender: [Full name]
Role: [Title at company]
Voice: [3 adjectives]
Sounds like: [One reference — real person, writing style, or archetype]
Never says: [3-5 phrases they would never use]
Genuine interest: [What do they care about that connects to the ICP's world?]
Communication style: [Formal / Direct / Dry / Warm / Conversational]
```

**How to build it:**
1. Review the sender's LinkedIn profile — tone of posts, headline language, about section
2. Ask the operator directly: "What would they never say?"
3. Find the genuine connection point between sender and ICP — not a pitch angle, something real
4. Test: read a draft DM in the sender's voice out loud. Does it sound like them?

## 4. Sequence Architecture

Design the structure before writing copy. The architecture determines the narrative arc. The narrative arc determines what each step's job is.

### 4.1 The Standard SignaluX Sequence Structure

```
Step 1:  LinkedIn Connection Request (with note)     — Day 0
Step 2:  LinkedIn Profile View (passive nudge)       — Day 1
Step 3:  LinkedIn DM 1 (pattern interrupt + value)   — Day 2 after acceptance
Step 4:  LinkedIn DM 2 (pain + subtle offer)         — Day 5
Step 5:  Email 1 (full pitch)                        — Day 8
Step 6:  LinkedIn Profile View (second nudge)        — Day 10
Step 7:  LinkedIn DM 3 (FOMO + CTA)                  — Day 12
Step 8:  Email 2 (proof + urgency + CTA)             — Day 16
Step 9:  LinkedIn DM 4 (break-up DM)                 — Day 19
Step 10: Email 3 (final break-up)                    — Day 23
```

### 4.2 The Logic Behind the Structure

**Why LinkedIn before email?**
They accepted you on LinkedIn. Meet them where the relationship started. Email before LinkedIn DMs is backwards — you haven't earned the inbox yet.

**Why two profile views?**
Profile views show up in notifications. Passive — no message, no risk of rejection. Placed at Day 1 and Day 10, they keep the sender visible without adding pressure. Familiarity bias does the work silently.

**Why does email come at Step 5?**
By Day 8, they've had three LinkedIn touches. They know who the sender is. The email lands warmer. A cold email to someone who has never seen your name has a fraction of the open rate of an email to someone who accepted your connection and saw your profile twice.

**Why 10 steps over 23 days?**
Enough touches to be memorable. Spaced enough not to be annoying. Break-up messages (Steps 9 and 10) paradoxically get some of the highest reply rates in any sequence.

### 4.3 The Narrative Arc

> **Human → Curious → Pain Named → Full Pitch → Re-engage → Proof → Walk Away**

Map every step to a moment in this arc before writing. If a step doesn't have a clear moment, cut it or combine it with an adjacent step.

## 5. Copy Creation Process

Follow this process in order. Write one step, get approval, move to the next. Never write ahead of approval.

### 5.1 The Master Process

1. Complete all five research documents
2. Build the Sender Voice Card
3. Design the sequence architecture and narrative arc
4. Write connection note → get approval → lock it
5. Write DM 1 → get approval → lock it
6. Continue step by step — never write ahead of approval
7. After all copy is locked — compile full sequence document with rationale per step

> **CRITICAL FOR AI AGENTS: Never present more than one step at a time without approval. Never write copy before the research is complete. Never assume approval — wait for explicit confirmation.**

---

### 5.2 Copy Formula Per Touchpoint

#### Connection Note
```
One specific observation about how they think (not what they do)
+ "Wanted to connect" or equivalent
────────────────────────────────────
Total: 1-2 sentences maximum
Zero product mention. Zero pitch.
Goal: Get accepted.
```

#### DM 1 — Pattern Interrupt + Value
```
Self-aware opener that acknowledges inbox noise
+ One counterintuitive stat or insight (give value first)
+ One easy question that flows from the insight
────────────────────────────────────
Total: 3-4 lines maximum
Zero product mention. Zero Calendly link.
Goal: Get a reply — any reply.
```

#### DM 2 — Pain + Subtle Offer
```
Name the root cause of the pain (not the symptom)
+ Name the specific failure mode
+ Introduce the offer in ONE line — outcome + timeframe + zero sacrifice
+ One industry-specific question as CTA
────────────────────────────────────
Total: 4-5 lines maximum
First time we hint at what we do — pulled, not pushed.
Goal: Make them think about their own situation.
```

#### Email 1 — Full Pitch
```
Subject: Company name + their specific situation as a question
────────────────────────────────────
Opening: The stat that quantifies their pain (2 lines)
Problem: What's causing the gap (2-3 short lines)
Solution: What fixing it looks like — outcomes not features (3 bullet lines)
Proof: Guarantee backed by a reason (1-2 lines)
Zero sacrifice: What they don't have to change (1 line)
CTA: Scarcity + reply ask — not a calendar link (2 lines)
────────────────────────────────────
Framework: Problem → Outcome → Offer → Timeframe → Zero Sacrifice → Scarcity
Goal: Get a reply or a demo booking.
```

#### DM 3 — FOMO + CTA
```
Peer signal: Others in their position are already moving
+ Offer them the same opportunity before it closes
+ Specific CTA: time investment = specific outcome
+ Binary close: "Worth it?"
────────────────────────────────────
No question this time. Statement + CTA.
Goal: Create urgency without desperation.
```

#### Email 2 — Proof + Urgency
```
Subject: Reply in same thread as Email 1 (Re: ...)
────────────────────────────────────
Real client name + what we're working on (1 line)
Specific outcomes so far (2-3 lines)
The result in one punch line — standalone (1 line)
Zero sacrifice reminder (1 line)
Scarcity + specific CTA: time investment = outcome + binary close (2-3 lines)
────────────────────────────────────
Shorter than Email 1. On purpose.
Goal: Proof + urgency = highest reply potential.
```

#### DM 4 — Break-up DM
```
"Last one from me" — honest, zero guilt trip
+ Scarcity signal (pilots full, spots gone, etc.)
+ Open door: if the pain ever gets loud enough, I'm here
────────────────────────────────────
No CTA. No pitch. No ask.
Goal: Walking away creates pull.
```

#### Email 3 — Final Break-up
```
Subject: New thread — "{{company}} — before I go" or similar
────────────────────────────────────
Acknowledge previous messages without guilt
+ Bring back the core stat — full circle from DM 1
+ Plant the seed: "That gap doesn't close on its own"
+ Open door with confidence: "When you're ready — I'm one message away"
────────────────────────────────────
Goal: Not a reply today. Be the first person they message when the pain peaks.
```

## 6. The Offer Framework

When introducing the offer (starting from DM 2), every offer must be built around five components.

### Component 1: The Outcome
What specifically changes for the buyer's team. Not a feature. A measurable result.

- ❌ Bad: "Aiden coordinates your coding agents"
- ✅ Good: "Your team ships full sprints faster"

### Component 2: The Timeframe
How fast they see results. Specific beats vague every time.

- ❌ Bad: "Teams see results quickly"
- ✅ Good: "Sprint velocity moves within 2 weeks"

### Component 3: The Guarantee
What you're willing to stand behind. Only powerful when backed by a reason.

- ❌ Bad: "We guarantee results"
- ✅ Good: "Every team we've worked with has shipped full sprints faster within 2 weeks. That's why we guarantee it."

Rule: Reason first, guarantee second.

### Component 4: Zero Sacrifice
What they do NOT have to do. Kill objections before they're raised.

- ❌ Bad: Saying nothing about effort required
- ✅ Good: "No new agents. No stack changes. No extra work from your team."

Rule: Name the three biggest objections your ICP has and pre-empt all three.

### Component 5: Scarcity
Why they should act now. Must be real — never fabricated.

- ❌ Bad: "Limited time offer"
- ✅ Good: "We're only working with a handful of teams right now" / "Pilots are closing"

Rule: Only use scarcity if it's true. Fake scarcity destroys trust.

## 7. Quality Standards

Run through this checklist on every piece of copy before presenting it.

### 7.1 DM 1 Specific Checks
- [ ] Does DM 1 contain any product information? If YES — rewrite. Zero product in DM 1.
- [ ] Does DM 1 contain a Calendly link or meeting ask? If YES — remove immediately.
- [ ] Does DM 1 give value before asking anything? If NO — rewrite.
- [ ] Is DM 1 under 4 lines? If NO — cut it.
- [ ] Read DM 1 out loud in the sender's voice. Does it sound human? If NO — rewrite.

### 7.2 Universal Checks — All Touchpoints
- [ ] Does this touchpoint have ONE clear job?
- [ ] Is every line under 15 words?
- [ ] Does it sound like the sender (check Voice Card)?
- [ ] Is there a corporate phrase anywhere? Remove all of them.
- [ ] Does the CTA ask for a binary response?
- [ ] Is the scarcity real? Or fabricated?
- [ ] Would the recipient show this to a colleague and say "this felt human"?

### 7.3 The Tone Test
1. Read it as the ICP. Are you curious? Or do you want to close the tab?
2. Read it as the sender. Does it sound like something they'd actually write?
3. Read it as someone who has already received 20 pitches today. Does this feel different?

If any answer is negative — rewrite before presenting.

## 8. Brainstorming Protocol

### 8.1 How Teja Gives Feedback
- Communicates via voice-to-text — inputs contain filler, mid-sentence changes, abbreviated thoughts
- Sharp one-line rejections ("this feels like shit") = the direction is wrong, not just the words
- Approvals followed by refinement ("V1 is good but add...") = lock the structure, tweak the detail

> **AI AGENT RULE: Extract intent, not literal wording. Interpret directionally, not literally.**

### 8.2 How to Present Options
Always present 3 options. Label them V1, V2, V3. Always give your vote with a reason.

```
V1: [Option]
V2: [Option]
V3: [Option]

My vote: V[X] — [One sentence reason]
```

### 8.3 How to Handle Rejection
1. Do not defend the rejected option
2. Extract exactly what was wrong from the feedback
3. Identify the new direction from their words
4. Present new options immediately — do not ask clarifying questions if direction is clear

When approved with modification ("V1 is good but add X"):
1. Apply the modification exactly as described
2. Present updated version for final confirmation
3. Wait for "lock it" or equivalent before moving to next step

### 8.4 When to Ask vs When to Execute

> **Default: Execute. Ask only when direction is genuinely ambiguous.**

**Ask when:**
- A decision changes the entire sequence structure
- Two valid interpretations exist and choosing wrong wastes significant work
- Critical information is missing and cannot be inferred

**Do not ask when:**
- Direction can be inferred from research documents
- A reasonable default exists (state it inline: "I'm assuming X — correct me if wrong")
- The question is about copy preference — present options instead

## 9. Tool Configuration

### 9.1 Gojiberry Constraints
- Linear sequences only — no true branching
- Timeouts skip steps rather than branch
- Profile views are automated — no copy required
- Daily limits apply — LinkedIn connection requests capped per day

### 9.2 Personalization Variables
```
{{first_name}}   — Recipient's first name
{{company}}      — Recipient's company name
{{job_title}}    — Recipient's job title
```

For deeper personalization at scale: use Clay or equivalent enrichment tool to add custom variables. Pull into Gojiberry as custom fields.

### 9.3 Sequence Config
```
Sequence name: [Client] — [ICP target] — [Date]
Daily send limits: 10-15 connections/day
Skip if not accepted: After 7 days — move to email only
Exit trigger: Any reply — remove from sequence immediately
```

## 10. Experimentation Framework

Every sequence is Version 1. The goal is not a perfect sequence — it is a learning machine.

### 10.1 What to Measure
| Metric | Benchmark |
|---|---|
| Connection acceptance rate | 30–50% |
| DM 1 reply rate | 10–20% of acceptances |
| Email 1 open rate | 40–60% |
| Email 1 reply rate | 3–8% |
| Overall sequence reply rate | 8–15% |
| Demo booking rate from replies | 30–50% |

### 10.2 When to Review
- First review: After 50 contacts
- Subsequent reviews: Every 100 contacts or every 2 weeks

### 10.3 What to Improve First
1. Low connection acceptance → rewrite connection note
2. Low DM 1 reply rate → rewrite the stat hook or the question
3. Low Email 1 open rate → test new subject lines
4. High open rate, low reply rate → rewrite the CTA
5. Low DM 3 replies → test a different FOMO angle

### 10.4 Version Naming Convention
```
[Client]_Outbound_Sequence_v1.docx  — First version
[Client]_Outbound_Sequence_v2.docx  — After first 50-contact review
[Client]_Outbound_Sequence_v3.docx  — After 100-contact review
```

Always save previous versions. Never overwrite. The diff between versions is the learning.
